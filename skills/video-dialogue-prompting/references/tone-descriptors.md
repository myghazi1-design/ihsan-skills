# Tone Descriptors Library

Place inside the character bracket after the role name, per the **P3 — Audio Details** principle. Never repeat the same descriptor across emotional beats — shift it when the emotion shifts.

## Emotional States

- controlled serious voice
- warm nostalgic voice
- shouting loudly
- voice cracking
- sharp defensive voice
- calm but threatening tone
- exhausted voice
- fast urgent voice
- hesitant voice
- soft tone
- whispering
- trembling frustrated voice
- confident authoritative voice
- sleepy amused voice
- annoyed sarcastic tone
- emotional voice
- cold quiet tone

## Physical Voice Qualities

- raspy, deep voice
- clear fearful voice
- breathy
- booming
- thin high voice
- gravelly
- smoky
- crisp articulate
- mumbling
- rapid-fire

## Usage pattern

```
[Character A: Lead Detective, controlled serious voice]: "Let's stop pretending."
[Character A: Lead Detective, calm but threatening tone]: "Then explain why your fingerprints are here."
```

Note the descriptor shift between beats — the detective moves from *controlled serious* to *calm but threatening* as the scene escalates. The label stays identical (P1); the descriptor shifts (P3).

## Combining

Emotional state + physical voice quality can be combined for richer characterization:

```
[Character A: Veteran, exhausted voice, gravelly]: "We should've turned back an hour ago."
```
